Anonymous Perpetrator Matching
⚖️ Court-grade design. This system is modelled on the Callisto standard — the only survivor matching architecture with a documented legal track record.
🔗 How matching works — step by step
1. Your input never leaves your device as plaintext.
Perpetrator identifiers (name, phone, email, social handle) are converted to a one-way cryptographic hash on your device before upload. The original text is never transmitted or stored.

2. A match only triggers at 2 or more independent reports.
A single submission — including any attempt to probe the system — returns nothing. Only when two or more survivors have independently submitted the same identifier does a match occur.

3. Notification goes to attorneys only — never directly to you or the other survivor.
When a match is confirmed, two separate pro bono attorneys are each notified — one per survivor. Each attorney receives only the contact details of their own survivor. Neither attorney is told the perpetrator's name. Neither survivor's identity is revealed to the other.

4. You choose what happens next.
Your attorney contacts you privately to explain your options. You decide whether to proceed, connect with the other survivor (with their consent), or take no action. The perpetrator is never notified at any stage.
⚠️ Why advocates cannot hold your evidence
Non-attorney advocates who receive or store survivor evidence can be compelled to testify in court — which can expose your identity and undermine your case. SafeTrace routes all match notifications through attorneys, who are protected by attorney-client privilege.
Your records are opted in to matching. If a match is confirmed at the 2+ threshold, a pro bono attorney will contact you privately.

Release a record
Only you can authorize a release. SafeTrace cannot produce vault contents in response to any third-party request — including law enforcement subpoenas.
⚖️ Which pathway is strongest in court?
Attorney release is the most legally protected. Your attorney receives your record under attorney-client privilege — it cannot be used against you and is shielded from opposing counsel without your consent.

Advocate release carries a risk. Non-attorney advocates who hold your evidence can be subpoenaed to testify, which may expose your identity and the contents of your record. Only release to an advocate if they are supervised by an attorney or your jurisdiction provides statutory advocate privilege.

Law enforcement release initiates an investigation. Once released, you lose control of how the record is used. Consult an attorney first.
